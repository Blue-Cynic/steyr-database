Development History

By the late 80s, Steyr decided to design another SMG, right after the Para and the MPi SMGs. Steyr decided to return to the almost pistol shape of the MPi SMGs. The head designer of this project would be Friedrich Aigner, one of the key people behind the GB pistol. Due to him heading the project, he decided to return to the delayed blowback system of the GB pistol and adapt it to this new SMG. After trials and tribulations, they came out with the TMP or Tactical Machine Pistol.

The weapon was very compact, even for its class, being essentially a somewhat large pistol in terms of size. Its body is made purely out of polymer, much like the AUG which made it very light weight. Its size and weight made it the perfect CQC gun. Steyr marketed the gun primarily to law enforcement for officers generally dealt with close range engagements.

Much like the MPi SMGs, the gun had a lukewarm reception. It was adopted in limited numbers by militaries around the world. In the end, from 1992 t0 2001, Steyr decided to simply sell the rights of the gun to another company and cease productions of the weapon. The company that bought the rights to the design would be Brugger and Thormet, a Swiss company known for making high end suppressors and other attachments. They would take the design and improve upon it, releasing the new SMG as the MP9. Compared to the TMP, the MP9 sells much better and is still in production. In fact, it overshadows the TMP that some people do not realize that the MP9 was a derivative and upgrade of another gun.

Notable Features

Lightweight

The weapon is very lightweight, making it a good choice for a weapon to be carrying around all day.

Compact

The weapon is compact, even with a suppressor attached. This makes it perfect for close quarters fighting.

High Rate of Fire

It has a blisteringly high rate of fire, perfect for close ranges when one needs to dump as many bullets as they can onto a target.
