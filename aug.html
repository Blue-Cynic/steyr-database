Development History

The Austrian military wanted to replace their StG 58 rifles (Austrian licensed FN FAL) due to its excessive recoil and power which was unnecessary for the average infantryman.

Following the USA's introduction of the 5.56mm NATO cartridge and the M16, Steyr Arms decided to adopt the cartridge due to its speed and performance. Rather than settling with the traditional rifle design like the M16 and StG 58, they went for a bullpup configuration in order to have a barrel as long as the M16's but much more compact than it while also being easily detachable.

Unlike with the M16, they went with a short stroke gas piston system instead of a direct impingement system in order for the weapon to handle the cold Austrian Alps. They also settled with having the weapon possess an integrated scope rather than iron sights.

Upon its introduction and adoption by the Austrians in 1977 as the StG 77, it was quite the futuristic weapon and advanced for its time. And even today, that is the first impression people get with the gun for it has aged well due to its reliability and Steyr's continued support of the weapon.

Variants and Modifications

  • AUG A1 - The first iteration of the rifle.
  • AUG A2 - Replaces the built-in scope with a picatinny rail for attaching other optics.
  • AUG A3 - Revamps the body, particularly the picatinny rail for optics mounting and added more picatinny rails around the weapon for better modularity with weapon attachments like lights and grips.
  • AUG Para - The SMG variant that is gas blowback operated and not gas piston operated.
  • AUG HBAR - The automatic rifle variant of the weapon. Features a longer and thicker barrel for sustained automatic fire and is often issued with 42 round magazines. Has a slower ROF and fires from an open bolt configuration.

Notable Features

Feature 1

The weapon has a quick detach barrel system. It allows easy swapping of barrels, perfect to adapt the weapon for varying mission profiles (20in barrels and longer for long range shooting to carbine barrels for close quarters fighting) and for barrel swaps with the HBAR variant.

Feature 2

Adjustable gas block for every barrel in order to tune the weapon's gas system to be comfortable and reliable to use based on ammunition used and if a suppressor is attached.

Feature 3

The weapon itself has a massive aftermarket parts community due to its popularity. The weapon is modular as is, but the aftermarket support for it makes it much more modular and appealing to own and use.
