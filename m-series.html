Development History

Work on the M series, particularly the Steyr M series began in the 1990s which was led by Austrian arms designer, Wilhelm Bubits. Mr. Bubits was a retired police officer and former Glock engineer so he knew a thing or two in designing handguns. Steyr's chief engineer, Friedrich Aigner would also be involved with the project. His previous design for Steyr was the Steyr TMP. The M line would be unveilved in 1999, a longside the S line. A thing to note about them which their successors also possess is a low bore axis. This is when the pistol barrel is much closer to the shooter's hand. For this reason, the guns have reduced muzzle flip and perceived recoil. These lines would last from 1999 up to 2004 of which the A1s would be introduced.

With the A1 lines introduced, it brought along two new sizes of the handgun, the L line which were the largest, and the C line, which is smaller than the M line, but not as small as the S line. The A1s were produced from 2004 up to 2010. The A1s would be reitarated upon with better triggers, magazines that can hold 2 more round through extended baseplates, and reversible magazine releases. Steyr decided not to change the name and these guns would still be considered A1s and would be produced for a decade.

By 2019, a new iteration would come out called the A2-MF line. They had more texturing for improved ergonomics and to grip the gun much better. They also have pictainny rails in order to mount lights and lasers on the gun, alongside swappable backstraps and side plates. The MF in this series stood for "Modular Frame".

Variants and Modifications

  • M line - The most popular. It is the "Goldilocks" in terms of size, not too big and not too small. It too has an A1 series which released along with the L and C series, but making an entry for them would be redundant.
    • M9 and M9-A1 - chambered in 9x19mm or 9x21mm
    • M40 and M40-A1 - chambered in 40 S&W
    • M357 and M357-A1 - chambered in .357 SIG
  • S line - The subcompact version. Smallest of the series. Also has an A1 series.
    • S9 and S9-A1 - chambered in 9x19mm
    • S40 and S40-A1 - chambered in 40 S&W
  • L line - The largest of the series, being a full-sized service pistol in terms of length. Was not introduced with the first M line. It appeared alongside the revamped M line and its other siblings.
    • L9-A1 - chambered in 9x19mm
    • L40-A1 - chambered in 40 S&W
  • C line - The compact version. It is slightly shorter than the M series. Same story of introduction like the L line
    • C9-A1 - chambered in 9x19mm or 9x21mm
    • C40-A1 - chambered in 40 S&W
  • A2 MF line - The newest of the series. Not backwards compatible with the previous lines. Only chambered in 9x19mm.
    • M9-A2 MF
    • L9-A2 MF
    • C9-A2 MF

Notable Features

Low Bore Axis

The weapon has a relatively low bore axis for a pistol. This means reduced muzzle flip and felt recoil.

Innovative Sights

Comes with trapezoid sights which can swapped for more tradiitonal sights or red dot sights; and the A2 line allows the use of many lights and lasers on the gun thanks to the picatinny rail system included with it.

Magazine Interchangability

The guns have interchangeable magazines across each iteration. So an M9-A2 MF can use a magazine from an M9 and an M9-A1 and vice versa. Just ensure the caliber of the magazine matches with the gun's caliber.
